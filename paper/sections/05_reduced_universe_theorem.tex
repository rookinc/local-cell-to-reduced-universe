\section{Reduced-universe derivation theorem}

Artifact 002 proves the central claim of this paper: the reduced 16-candidate universe is generated by taking two readouts of the Project 22 local-cell kernel.

Let
\[
    L=\{O0,O1,B0,B1\}
\]
be the Project 22 local answer-cell kernel.  The C readout is
\[
    C(L)=[O0,O1,B0,B1],
\]
and the anchor readout is
\[
    A(L)=[B0,O0,O1,B1].
\]
The reduced visible universe is
\[
    U=C(L)\times A(L),
\]
with candidate index
\[
    i=4c+a.
\]

\begin{theorem}[Local-cell-to-reduced-universe theorem]
Given the Project 22 local answer-cell kernel \(L\), the two readouts \(C(L)\) and \(A(L)\) generate exactly 16 visible candidates, indexed \(0,\ldots,15\).  The hidden identity condition
\[
    x_C=x_A
\]
selects exactly candidates
\[
    [1,6,8,15].
\]
In visible coordinates this identity selector appears as the permutation
\[
    [1,2,0,3]=(0\ 1\ 2)(3).
\]
\end{theorem}

\paragraph{Proof.}
The product of two four-state readouts has \(4\times4=16\) candidates.  Because the two readouts contain the same hidden states, the identity selector contributes one accepted candidate for each state.  The anchor positions of the C-ordered states are
\[
    O0\mapsto 1,\quad
    O1\mapsto 2,\quad
    B0\mapsto 0,\quad
    B1\mapsto 3.
\]
Therefore the visible permutation is
\[
    [1,2,0,3].
\]
Using \(i=4c+a\), the selected candidates are
\[
    4\cdot0+1=1,\quad
    4\cdot1+2=6,\quad
    4\cdot2+0=8,\quad
    4\cdot3+3=15.
\]
Thus the selected set is exactly
\[
    [1,6,8,15].
\]
